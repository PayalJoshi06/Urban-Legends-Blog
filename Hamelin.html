Overview

The Pied Piper of Hamelin is a legend originating from the town of Hamelin (Hameln), Lower Saxony, Germany, in the 13th century.

It tells the story of a piper hired by the town to rid it of rats, but when the townspeople refuse to pay him, he takes revenge by luring away their children.

Story

According to the legend, in 1284, the town of Hamelin was infested with rats. A mysterious piper appeared and offered to rid the town of the rats in exchange for payment.

The piper played his flute, and all the rats followed him to the Weser River, where they drowned. However, when the townspeople refused to pay the agreed amount, the piper vowed revenge.

On June 26th, the piper returned and played his flute again, this time luring away all the town's children, except for one who was deaf and couldn't hear the music.

Legend

The legend of the Pied Piper of Hamelin has been retold in various forms over the centuries, with different interpretations and endings.

Some versions suggest that the children were led to a cave or enchanted mountain, never to be seen again, while others claim they were taken to Transylvania or were drowned in the Weser River.

Legacy

The story of the Pied Piper of Hamelin has become a popular subject in literature, music, art, and theater.

It serves as a cautionary tale about the consequences of not honoring promises and the importance of gratitude and generosity.
