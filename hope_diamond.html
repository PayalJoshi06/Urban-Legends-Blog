History of The Hope Diamond

The Hope Diamond's history can be traced back to its discovery in India, where it was originally a large blue diamond known as the Tavernier Blue. Over the centuries, it changed hands numerous times and gained its current name in the 19th century when it was acquired by Henry Philip Hope.

The history of the Hope Diamond can be traced back to the Kollur mine in Golconda, India, where it was originally discovered. The rough diamond, known as the Tavernier Blue, weighed about 112 carats in its uncut state. It was purchased by French merchant Jean-Baptiste Tavernier in the mid-17th century and subsequently sold to King Louis XIV of France in 1668.

Under Louis XIV's orders, the diamond was recut into a 67-carat, deep blue gem and became known as the "French Blue." It adorned the French crown jewels until it was stolen during the French Revolution in 1792.

The diamond disappeared for several years before resurfacing in London, where it was recut and sold to various owners. It eventually came into the possession of Henry Philip Hope, a British banker, in the early 19th century. It was during this time that the diamond acquired its current name, the "Hope Diamond."

The Hope Diamond changed hands multiple times over the years, passing through various royal families and private collectors. In 1958, jeweler Harry Winston donated the diamond to the Smithsonian Institution, where it remains on display to this day as one of the most famous gems in the world.
